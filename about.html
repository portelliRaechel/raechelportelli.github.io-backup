My geospatial journey

I am a geographer. Like most geographers, I have an eclectic collection of interests. The motivation for my research is the use of remote sensing. I have leveraged machine learning methods for extracting information from remote sensing data, studied human factors in remote sensing image use, and have a keen interest in the history of geospatial technology development. The following is brief narrative about my journey.

early years

I began my journey down the path of Geography in 2004 at Northern Michigan University. My initial academic endeavor took me to the Hogeschool van Utrecht in the Netherlands in 2000 where I studied Integrated Communications and Marketing. Neither topic held my interest. Geography, on the other hand, fully captured my imagination. As an undergraduate student, I triple majored in Physical Geography, Geology, and Environmental Science (Social Science Option) at the University of Idaho. I quickly grew fond of geomorphology and remote sensing. Throughout my time at the University of Idaho, I worked as an undergraduate research assistant and a geology, geography, and statistics tutor.

The birth of my son at the end of my Junior year meant stepping back from my geology major, but I remained at the University of Idaho to obtain my master’s degree in Geography in one year. My topic was discriminant analysis for tree species classification from moderate-resolution (SPOT-5) imagery in Central Idaho.

After graduating with my master’s degree, I took up my studies at the Pennsylvania State University, working with eminent geovisualization scholar Alan MacEachren. While at Penn State, I was fortunate enough to be introduced to cognitive GIScience. Working with Alan strongly influenced my perspective on remote sensing imagery interpretation. My dissertation addressed the role of domain expertise in the decision-making processes of interpreters during forest disturbance attribution using an interactive image analysis tool.

middle years

Upon conferral my Ph.D. I took a position at Michigan State University in the Geography Department. There I expanded my remote sensing analytics skillset to object-based image analysis. I mentored a number of graduate and undergraduate students in their pursuit of geospatial research. In addition to publishing a number of peer reviewed journal articles on remote sensing, I also led an international team in the development of the book Remote Sensing and Cognition: Human Factors in Image. I made the decision to leave the university in 2021 to pursue a career path with a better work-life balance.

now

In Summer 2022 I joined the Department of Geography and Geographic Information Science at the University of Illinois Urbana Champaign. I have begun to pivot my research to Trust and geospatial AI in remote sensing applications. In addition to my research, I develop and instruct a variety of Python-driven geospatial analytics courses to graduate and undergraduate students.
