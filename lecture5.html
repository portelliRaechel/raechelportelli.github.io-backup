Geographic Coordinate System

system of numbers (latitude, longitude) associated with locations on the earth.

Planar vs Polar Coordinates

Polar coordinates utilize the angular relaltionship between two points to describe location. Planar coordinates, or Cartesian coordniates utilize a grid system.

Geodetic Datum

coordinate system with a reference surface (ie. sea level) that provides known locations for creating maps

Projection

algorithm for translating spherical coordinates into a 2-dimensional representation.

UTM

  • Global
  • Covers 80 degrees north to 80 degrees south latitude
  • Comprised of 60 6 degree zones
  • Each zoness tapers from 666,000m at the equator to 70m at the pole
  • distortion is limited to 1 prt in 1,000 for each zone
  • for large areas multiple coordinate systems are used

SPCS

  • Local to the United States
  • 124 zones across all of the states
  • each zone is based on a specific projection
  • distortion is no more thn 1 part in 10,000
  • Multiple systems are used in individual states

Michigan GeoRef System

  • Local to Michigan
  • Seperate from the SPCS
  • One zone for the entirty of the state
  • Uses a Oblique Mercator projection

Projection Surfaces

A geometric shape that can be laid out into a flat surface without stretching or tearing.


  • Conic
  • Cylindrical
  • Azimuthal

Planar Orientation

Orientation of the projection surface in relation to the globe


  • Normal- parallel to the earth's axis
  • Transverse- perpendicular to the earht's axis
  • Oblique- Other orientation

Projection Parameters

  • Center Point
  • Standard Parallell

Impact of Standard Lines

Standard lines define the relationship of the surface to the spheroid.

  • Tangent-surface touches and does not intersect.
  • Secant-surface intersects with the spheroid.

Projection Review

Map Projection Properties

  • Area- preservation of area (equivalency)
  • Distance- preservation of distance, but only in one direction
  • Direction- preservation of direction
  • Form- preservation of the shape of features (conformal)
  • Compromise- balance distortion

Equivalent Map Projections

  • Dot Density
  • Choropleth
  • When size comparison is important

Conformal Projections

  • Accurate local directions
  • Shapes of reatures or contour lines
  • Navigational charts
  • Note: Many large scale maps are conformal

Equidistant Map Projections

  • To show how far one location is from another
  • Airline Maps
  • Earthquake maps

Compromise Map Projections

  • Reference Maps (shape and area)

How to choose a good map projection?

  • Preserve the most important spatial property
  • Minimize distortion in your area of interest
