Introduction

Visualization

First coined in 1953 by Allen K. Philbrick. It was not until 1987 when the term took on its current definition under the National Science Foundation

Visualization is the representation of an object, situation, or set of information as a chart or other image.

What is cartography?

  • Cartography is the discipline dealing with the art, science and technology of making and using maps (ICA).

What is a map?

  • A map is a symbolized representation of geographical reality, representing selected features or characteristics, resulting from the creative effort of its author’s execution of choices, and is designed for use when spatial relationships are of primary relevance (ICA).

Geovisualization

  • the process of interactively visualizing geographic information in any of the steps in spatial analyses, even though it can also refer to the visual output, or the associated techniques.(BoK)

Geovisualization

Geovisualization emerged in 1995, MacEachren and Kraak differentiate geovisualization from scientific visualization as an integration of ViSC along with approaches from cartography, image analysis, information visualization, exploratory data analysis, and GIS. Although the term geovisualization is often first linked to this period, there are a number of other researchers in the 1980s that linked visualization to GIS and cartography (see McCormick 1987).

Geovisual Analytics

  • the science of analytical reasoning with spatial information as facilitated by interactive visual interfaces. (BoK)

Geovisual Analytics

During the early 2000s a similar transformation occurs when the field of visual analyticsemerges (Thomas and Cook 2005). Soon after, geographers adopt this approach for geospatial problems (see Andrienko et al. 2007).

What can't a "map" do?

  • Maps are not photographs. They are selective and symbolic.
  • Maps are not absolute. They reflect the biases of the cartographer.
  • Maps are reduced. They are scaled down from reality.
  • Maps are distorted. They are 2-D representations of 3-D earth.*
  • Maps are spatial. Maps are only good for representing spatial info.

The Cartographic Process

  1. Determine the purpose and scope for your map
  2. Gather and analyze the necessary data
  3. Map your data to visual elements
  4. Implement and refine your design

Planning

  • Clearly define the problem
  • Identify the stakeholders
  • Outline objectives
  • Determine expected end product
  • Acknowledge constraints

Preparing

  • Identify potential resources
  • Weigh feasibility, appropriateness, and authority of sources.
  • Obtain and clean
  • Analyze
  • Execute data management and documentation

Encoding

  • Determine an appropriate visualization
  • Select appropriate variables for representation
  • Choose appropriate visualization primitives

Implementing and Refinement

  • Develop prototype visualization
  • Perform UX research as possible
  • Improve design
  • Implement design
